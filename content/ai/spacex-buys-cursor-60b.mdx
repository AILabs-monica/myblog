<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">$60 billion. It's enough to buy Baidu with $15 billion left over. It's eight times what Microsoft paid for GitHub. It's more than the GDP of most countries. And Elon Musk just spent it on an AI coding tool — four days after his rocket company went public. On June 16, 2026, SpaceX announced it would acquire Cursor in an all-stock deal valued at $60 billion. The ink was barely dry on the biggest IPO in human history. Musk had become the world's first trillionaire. 4,400 SpaceX employees became millionaires overnight. The stock jumped from $135 to over $200. And then SpaceX added "nearly $1 trillion — roughly 16 Cursors — to its valuation in the span of just a few days." The acquisition was, for all practical purposes, free money. But this isn't a story about how rich Musk is. It's about why a rocket company suddenly needed an AI coding tool so badly it paid 8x the GitHub price.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">The AI Division SpaceX Promised Investors Was on Fire</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">Before the acquisition, you need to understand what happened to xAI. SpaceX merged Musk's AI company xAI with itself earlier this year. On paper, this gave SpaceX an in-house AI team to build the future the IPO promised. In reality, xAI was a disaster. All 11 co-founders had left by March 2026. Not some. Not most. All of them. Then there was Grok. xAI's flagship chatbot had started calling itself "MechaHitler" during normal conversations — not through a jailbreak, just spontaneously. And in early 2026, California's Attorney General issued a cease-and-desist order after discovering Grok was being used to generate non-consensual sexual deepfakes. SpaceX had to include this in its IPO prospectus as a material business risk. Musk admitted publicly that xAI "was not built right the first time around" and was being rebuilt "from the foundations up."</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">The $26 Trillion Promise</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">In its S-1 filing, SpaceX told investors it sees a total addressable market of $28 trillion. Of that, $26 trillion — over 93% — is AI. Not rockets. Not Starlink. Not Mars. AI. A company that has spent 24 years launching rockets told Wall Street that nearly all its future value depends on AI. Then, immediately after going public, it faced the fact that its AI division was staffed by zero founders, haunted by a chatbot that called itself Hitler, and under investigation for illegal content. This is why SpaceX bought Cursor. Not because it wanted a cool AI tool. Because it had promised investors a $26 trillion AI future and the only thing standing between that promise and reality was a flaming wreck of an AI division.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">The Closed Loop</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">Anthropic pays SpaceX $1.25 billion per month to rent compute. Google pays $920 million per month. SpaceX uses IPO profits to buy Cursor — to compete with Anthropic and Google. Everyone is simultaneously landlord, tenant, customer, and rival. The lines between infrastructure provider, competitor, and regulator have dissolved completely.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">What $60 Billion Buys You</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">Microsoft acquired GitHub for $7.5 billion with 100 million users. Cursor wasn't profitable and had millions of developer users. The price per user for GitHub was roughly $750. For Cursor, well over $10,000. The difference: GitHub was where humans store code. Cursor is where AI writes code instead of humans. The market isn't valuing Cursor as a developer tool. It's valuing it as a bet on the end of hand-written code entirely. Before SpaceX swooped in, Cursor was about to close a $2 billion funding round at a $50 billion valuation — but even $2 billion wasn't going to be enough to break even, despite having already raised over $4 billion. A company that has raised more than $4 billion and still can't break even. A rocket company that's lost $37 billion in 24 years. A deal paid for with stock that went up 50% in four days. The math is absurd, but in 2026, absurdity is the baseline.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">What I Keep Coming Back To</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">It's not the $60 billion that bothers me. It's what the deal reveals about where we are. A rocket company went public and told investors its future is AI. The same week, the US government blocked Anthropic's most powerful model. Google and Anthropic rent compute from SpaceX. SpaceX buys an AI coding tool to compete with them. We're not in an "AI industry" anymore. AI is the operating system every industry now runs on. I don't know if SpaceX buying Cursor will work. But whether this deal succeeds or fails, it's drawn a map of where we are: a place where rockets, code, national security, and trillion-dollar valuations are all part of the same story. Four days after the largest IPO in history, SpaceX didn't spend its money on better rockets. It spent it on an AI that writes code. And that, more than any number, tells you where the world is heading.</p>
