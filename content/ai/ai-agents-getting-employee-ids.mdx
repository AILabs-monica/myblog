<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">I read a number this week that stopped me mid-sentence. McKinsey — the most prestigious consulting firm on Earth — now operates 25,000 AI agents alongside its 60,000 human employees. That's not a pilot. That's roughly one AI for every two and a half humans. Goldman Sachs tested the AI coding agent Devin as a formal new employee last year — complete with onboarding, access permissions, and code review requirements. India's TCS, a 600,000-employee IT services giant, had its chairman publicly state that AI agents will eventually "rival the company's workforce in size." And then a question hit me: do these AI agents have employee IDs? How do they log into company systems? If an agent goes rogue — or just gets deprecated — how do you revoke its access across a hundred different internal tools? That's not a thought experiment. That's an operational emergency for companies that deployed AI agents first and thought about governance second.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">The Founding Team That Got Investors to Write $66M</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">NewCore emerged from stealth on June 15, 2026 with a founding team that reads like a cybersecurity dream roster: Zohar Alon (CEO) previously founded Dome9 (acquired by Check Point). Amihai Neiderman (CTO) was a research leader in Israel's Unit 8200. Erez Yarkoni (CCO) served as CIO of T-Mobile USA and Telstra. These are not people building a startup because it's fun. These are people who've run identity infrastructure for companies with tens of millions of users and seen exactly where the cracks are.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">Alon's origin story is refreshingly honest. He was reviewing the technology budget of a company that used a major identity provider. After seeing the size of the bill — millions of dollars annually — he assumed the customer must be thrilled. The customer's response: "No, I'm not." The market was big, stagnant, and ready to be disrupted.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">The Identity Crisis</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">Traditional IAM systems (Okta, Entra) were designed for humans. One person = one account. Static permissions by role. Lifecycle measured in months. Authentication via password + MFA. At human pace. Now contrast with AI agents: they create and destroy temporary credentials in seconds. They run 24/7. They need per-task, not per-role, access. They process at machine speed. Legacy platforms break at every layer. NewCore's approach is split-key architecture — credentials split between customer and platform, no single point of compromise — with human oversight via mobile app for grant, review, and revoke.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">The Inevitability Question</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">What struck me most wasn't the $66 million or the $300 million valuation. It was how Alon ended the interview. AI agents becoming a significant part of the workforce, he said, is "inevitable. The question is whether we're going to build the guardrails in time." Guardrails. Not "should we let AI agents in" — that ship sailed when McKinsey deployed 25,000 of them. The question is: when they're in, how do we control them?</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">I don't know what a 50/50 human-AI workforce looks like. I don't know what happens to office culture when half your colleagues never sleep, never take vacation, and can be deleted with a single API call. But I do know one thing: when someone raises $66 million to solve the problem of "how does an AI agent log into your company's email," the future has already arrived. The robots are already on payroll.</p>
