<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">I used to think big tech strategy announcements were someone else's problem. Then last week happened. 5 million people now use OpenAI's Codex every single week. That's up 400% from the beginning of the year. They're not playing with a chatbot. They're delegating work.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">Move One: Acquiring Ona — So AI Can Work While You Sleep</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">Ona is not a household name. For years, they've helped developers move coding environments from local laptops into the cloud, serving 2 million developers. Why does this matter for Codex? Because right now, every AI agent only works while you're watching. Close your laptop, the agent stops. Ona's technology changes that. Post-acquisition, Codex agents run inside a customer's own cloud environment — securely, persistently, with full logging, audit trails, and permission controls. Ona's CEO put it best: <strong>"Agents need more than intelligence; they need a trusted workspace."</strong> Intelligence without infrastructure is a demo. Intelligence with persistent infrastructure is a workforce.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">Move Two: The Oracle Handshake — Walking Through the Front Door of Every Enterprise</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">A friend in IT at a traditional bank wanted to buy AI services. It took three months — not because of capability, but because of procurement: "This vendor isn't on our approved list. We need three competitive bids." The Oracle deal solves this at scale. Tens of thousands of enterprises already have Oracle cloud commitments with pre-negotiated terms, approved billing, and security certifications. Now they can apply those credits toward OpenAI models. No new contract. No new vendor qualification. No legal review. Oracle's customer base — financial services, healthcare, government, manufacturing — are exactly where AI adoption has been slowest because procurement friction is massive. OpenAI just deleted that friction.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">Move Three: The IPO That Isn't (Yet)</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">OpenAI's S-1 filing announcement was two paragraphs: "We have not decided on timing yet; it may be a while because there are things we want to do that are likely easier as a private company." This is a power move dressed as a disclosure. By filing confidentially, OpenAI has given itself an option — not an obligation — to go public. The filing is a call option on the public markets. Meanwhile, SpaceX just pulled off the largest IPO in history. Anthropic is in IPO process with valuations exceeding $100 billion. FAANG is dead. Long live MANGOS: Meta, Anthropic, Nvidia, Google, OpenAI, SpaceX.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">The Big Picture: A Strategy Stacked Three Layers Deep</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">Layer 1: Persistent execution. Ona gives Codex the ability to run continuously in customer-controlled cloud environments. Layer 2: Enterprise distribution. Oracle gives instant access to tens of thousands of enterprise accounts with compliant billing. Layer 3: Capital readiness. The S-1 gives OpenAI financial optionality to accelerate whenever strategic.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">Against the chaos of Anthropic's models being shut down by the US government, OpenAI's moves look almost boringly methodical. No dramatic confrontations. No public sparring about safety philosophy. Just methodical execution: acquire the infrastructure, partner for distribution, file for financial flexibility. While everyone else fights about safety, OpenAI is building the pipes.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">What This Means: AI tools are crossing a threshold from "interesting to try" to "necessary for work." Platform lock-in is real and accelerating. And the more capable AI becomes at execution, the more valuable human judgment becomes. Direction, taste, standards — these become premium skills. OpenAI's triple move suggests they've chosen which game they're playing. They're not trying to be the best model. They're trying to be the best infrastructure. And infrastructure, once built, is very hard to displace.</p>
