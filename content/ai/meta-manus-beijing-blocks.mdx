<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">I've been watching the AI industry closely for years. I've seen hype cycles, funding frenzies, model releases. But last week, something shifted. It wasn't a model launch. It was six days of government interventions that told me one thing: the AI industry just crossed a line it can't uncross.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">June 9: Anthropic launches Claude Fable 5. June 10: Dario Amodei publicly calls for FAA-style regulation. June 12: The US government uses exactly that power — Fable 5 and Mythos 5 are forcibly removed. The CEO asked to be regulated. The government said yes. And the first thing they regulated was him. Even more unsettling: TechCrunch reported Amazon CEO Andy Jassy had privately raised concerns about Anthropic to regulators before the crackdown. Amazon — Anthropic's largest investor — was apparently working the back channels against its own portfolio company.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">June 13: Then came the Manus story. Kate Park at TechCrunch broke it: Meta had agreed to acquire Manus, a Chinese AI agent startup, for $2 billion. But Beijing stepped in and demanded the deal be unwound. Meta is now reportedly moving to comply. Think about the reversal. For twenty years, the script was: the US blocks Chinese companies from acquiring American technology. Now, China is blocking an American company from acquiring Chinese AI technology. The technological gravity has shifted.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">Manus builds autonomous AI agents — systems that navigate browsers, call APIs, complete multi-step tasks on their own. Every major tech company wants this capability. Meta's Llama models are strong, but turning a language model into an agent is a different problem. Buying Manus was the fastest path. Beijing's calculus was different. AI agent technology maps directly onto capabilities with strategic value. Two years ago, this deal would have gone through with champagne and press releases. In 2026, AI is a national asset. You don't sell national assets to foreign competitors.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">When you stack these six days side by side, a pattern emerges: AI is no longer a technology race. It's a border dispute. The question isn't "whose model is smarter." It's "whose model is allowed to run where." During the Cold War, Western nations created COCOM to prevent strategic technologies from reaching the Soviet bloc. We're watching the same architecture get rebuilt for AI — except both sides are building walls simultaneously.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">So what do you do? Three things are no longer optional. One: treat your AI supply chain like a physical one. The question needs to be "what happens to my product if this model disappears tomorrow?" Two: multi-model isn't optimization, it's insurance. Three: geopolitical compliance is now a product requirement, not a legal afterthought. Where you're incorporated, who your investors are, where your data lives — every dimension can trigger a government's regulatory radar.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">I started this thinking I was writing about a weird acquisition getting blocked. I ended up watching an industry cross a threshold it didn't see coming. The AI geopolitical era didn't start with a declaration. It started with six days in June, when both sides of the Pacific decided, simultaneously, that AI was too important to leave to the market. We're all living in the after.</p>
