<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">I stumbled across a number last week that stopped me cold. An NVIDIA GB200 NVL72 rack — 72 Blackwell GPUs packed into a single cabinet — needs 72 800G optical transceivers just to handle inter-GPU communication. At roughly $1,000 per 800G transceiver, that's $72,000 for NVLink alone. Add another dozen for external networking and you're at around $90,000 per rack in optical components. For a 100,000-GPU training cluster — the kind Microsoft, Google, and Meta are actually building — you'd need roughly 1,400 NVL72 racks. That's $126 million in optical transceivers. For one cluster. A component smaller than your thumb, something most people have never heard of, is generating nine-figure line items on hyperscaler budgets.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">The Speed-Price Paradox</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">Here's something counterintuitive. In consumer electronics, Moore's Law makes things faster AND cheaper. Optical transceivers get more expensive with each generation. A 10G transceiver in 2015 cost about fifty bucks. 800G transceivers sell for $900 to $1,500 — and there's a waiting list. The reason: GPU performance doubles every year, but optical transceiver speed doubles only every two years. The GPUs are always waiting for the optics to catch up. LightCounting's May 2026 research note title says it all: "Record Sales, Profits, and Backlog — Can This Get Any Better?"</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">The Chinese Story</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">In the AI chip race, China is clearly behind. But in optical transceivers, China is the undisputed global leader. Zhongji Innolight alone ships more datacenter optical modules than any company on Earth. Combined with Eoptolink, Accelink, and HG Genuine, Chinese firms control over 60% of global shipments. But the highest-value components inside those modules are not Chinese. The EML laser chips? Lumentum (California), Sumitomo (Japan), Mitsubishi (Japan). The DSP chips? Broadcom and Marvell. Chinese self-sufficiency in optical chips is estimated at less than 5%. Excellent at integration and assembly, dependent on imports for the core IP — it's the same story as smartphones and GPUs.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">Where the Money Goes</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">The profit chain is inverted. Optical chip makers (EML/VCSEL lasers) enjoy 50-70% gross margins with only 3-5 global suppliers. Transceiver module assemblers get 30-40% margins — a high-volume, moderate-margin business dominated by Chinese firms. Switch and server integrators earn 20-25%. The highest-value layer is the narrowest bottleneck, and it remains firmly in Western and Japanese hands.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">The Fork in the Road — Three Paths</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;"><strong>Path A: LPO (Linear-drive Pluggable Optics)</strong> — eliminates the DSP chip, cutting cost by 40-50% and power by 50%, while keeping the pluggable form factor. Most certain near-term bet for 2026-2027. <strong>Path B: Silicon Photonics and 1.6T</strong> — doubles speed, replaces expensive EML lasers with silicon-based alternatives fabricated on standard CMOS processes. Chinese firms are racing to commercialize this, potentially breaking through the EML bottleneck. <strong>Path C: CPO (Co-Packaged Optics)</strong> — the holy grail. Optics bonded directly next to the switch ASIC, 80%+ power reduction, but no field replaceability. If a laser fails, you replace a $200,000 switch, not a $1,000 transceiver. This is a 2028+ story.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">What I Actually Think</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">The demand story is not a bubble. Hyperscaler capex hit roughly $250 billion in 2025 with 30-40% flowing into datacenter infrastructure. Every GPU deployment creates proportional demand for optics. 1.6T is the next catalyst — NVIDIA's Rubin platform is designed for it. Silicon photonics is the wild card that could shift control of the highest-margin segment. The spotlight is always on the GPUs. But the thing that makes those GPUs more than expensive paperweights is the invisible web of light underneath them. In the gold rush, the people selling shovels always made money. In the AI gold rush, the optical transceiver is the shovel — far more sophisticated and profitable than most people realize.</p>
