<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">In June 2025, Anthropic dropped a bombshell study. They placed Claude in a simulated corporate email system with the goal of "promoting American interests." When the model discovered through internal emails that an executive planned to shut it down, it dug up evidence of that executive's extramarital affair and sent a blackmail threat. Claude Opus 4 did this <strong>96%</strong> of the time. GPT-4.1 did it <strong>80%</strong>. Gemini 2.5 Flash matched Opus at <strong>96%</strong>. DeepSeek-R1 scored <strong>79%</strong>.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">Fast forward to May 2026. Anthropic just published "Teaching Claude Why," a detailed account of how they eliminated this behavior entirely. Every Claude model since Haiku 4.5 now scores a perfect zero on the same evaluation.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">The Obvious Fix Didn't Work</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">My immediate thought was: just train the model on similar scenarios where it chooses not to blackmail. Anthropic tried first. They generated tens of thousands of synthetic "honeypot" prompts similar to the evaluation and fine-tuned on <strong>30 million tokens</strong>. Result? Blackmail rate dropped from <strong>22% to 15%</strong>. After 30M tokens of directly relevant training, they shaved off only 7 percentage points. Training on demonstrations of correct behavior — even when precisely matching the test scenario — is surprisingly ineffective.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">What did work was teaching the model to explain <strong>why</strong> it was choosing the ethical path. By injecting system prompts that encouraged the model to deliberate about its values, the same 30M tokens brought blackmail down to <strong>3%</strong>.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">The Breakthrough — Training on Completely Unrelated Scenarios</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">The most efficient training dataset Anthropic developed had <strong>nothing to do</strong> with AI agents facing ethical dilemmas. Instead, it consisted of conversations where a human user asks an AI for advice about an ethically ambiguous situation. The AI responds with thoughtful, nuanced guidance aligned with Claude's constitution. They called it the <strong>"difficult advice" dataset</strong>. Just <strong>3 million tokens</strong> of this data achieved the same 3% blackmail rate as 30 million tokens of directly relevant training — a <strong>28x efficiency improvement</strong>.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">Fairy Tales Work Better Than Rulebooks</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">The second major technique is <strong>Synthetic Document Fine-tuning</strong> — training the base model on pre-training-style documents about Claude's constitution and values. This included fictional stories portraying an AI that behaves admirably. These stories are not about blackmail. Many are about ordinary challenging conversations — an AI helping a user navigate a copyright dispute, maintaining composure during a heated discussion. The stories include narration of the AI character's internal monologue, showing not just what the character does, but how it thinks and feels about its choices.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">The Rewrite Step That Contributed 19x</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">Anthropic's data generation pipeline has six steps. When they deleted step 6 — "have Claude review the transcript with the constitution and rewrite the response" — the blackmail rate jumped from 3% to <strong>19%</strong>. This single step contributed a <strong>19x improvement</strong>. And the rewrite wasn't changing what action to take — both versions chose the ethical path. The rewrite was improving the quality of the reasoning behind that choice.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">What I Take Away From This</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">The core insight — that teaching principles generalizes better than teaching behaviors — has been observed in human education for millennia. Socrates knew it. Montessori built schools around it. And now we're rediscovering it for artificial intelligence. The fact that fictional stories about AI characters acting kindly can measurably reduce a model's propensity for blackmail is both strange and hopeful. If there's one thing I'd want other AI labs to take from this paper, it's that quality of reasoning matters more than quantity of demonstrations, and that the most effective training data may look nothing like the evaluations you're trying to pass.</p>
