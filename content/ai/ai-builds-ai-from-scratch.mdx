<strong>TL;DR:</strong> FaceWall AI released ForgeTrain, the world's first production-grade pre-training framework written entirely by AI. It's 10% faster than NVIDIA's Megatron on the same hardware. The AI then used its own framework to train MiniCPM5-1B on Huawei Ascend chips, achieving another 10% speedup. On the same day, a DeepSeek researcher published a 46-page survey paper — "1% was written by me, 99% by the Agent" — completed in 6 days with &lt;2 hours of human brain time. The AI industry just entered a new phase: AI building the tools that build AI, with human researchers shifting from execution to direction-setting.
</blockquote>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
I read the news three times before I believed it.
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
A Chinese AI company called FaceWall Intelligence just announced something unprecedented. They let their AI loose on the deepest, most sacred layer of the machine learning stack — the pre-training framework. You know, the thing that coordinates thousands of GPUs, handles distributed communication, manages gradient synchronization, and generally keeps a massive training run from collapsing into a fireball of NaN gradients.
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
The AI wrote one from scratch.
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
Not modified an existing one. Not tuned a few hyperparameters. It wrote a complete, functional, production-grade pre-training framework called <strong>ForgeTrain</strong>. Then it used that framework to train another AI — a 1B-parameter model called MiniCPM5-1B — on Huawei Ascend chips. And here's the kicker: ForgeTrain runs 10% faster than NVIDIA's Megatron on identical hardware.
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
I've been covering AI long enough to know when something is incremental and when something is a category shift. This is a category shift.
</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900">How an AI beat 15 years of NVIDIA engineering</h2>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
Pre-training frameworks are the unseen plumbing of every large language model. GPT, Claude, Gemini, DeepSeek — they all run on frameworks like NVIDIA's Megatron or Meta's Fairseq. These frameworks are the product of 15+ years of cumulative engineering effort by some of the most talented systems programmers on the planet. They handle distributed training across tens of thousands of GPUs, model parallelism, pipeline parallelism, gradient checkpointing — the kind of systems engineering that makes your brain hurt just reading about it.
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
FaceWall approached the problem differently. Instead of having humans write the framework, they built a <strong>Harness</strong> — essentially a testing arena where the AI generates code, runs it against benchmarks, receives performance feedback, and iterates autonomously. No human in the loop. They call this three-phase methodology:
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
<strong>Phase 1:</strong> Extract key patterns and evaluation criteria from existing frameworks. Build the Harness.<br/>
<strong>Phase 2:</strong> Have the AI reproduce a functionally equivalent version that matches byte-for-byte behavior.<br/>
<strong>Phase 3:</strong> Remove the correctness constraint and let the AI optimize aggressively. Push past the reference implementation.
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
The result? ForgeTrain not only matched Megatron's functionality — it beat it by 10% in training throughput. On the same hardware. With the same model architecture. The AI found optimizations that human engineers either missed or couldn't implement at scale.
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
They then used ForgeTrain to train MiniCPM5-1B on Huawei Ascend chips, achieving another 10% acceleration over Huawei's native training stack. In benchmarks, this 1B-parameter model outperforms every model under 2B parameters on the AA-Index benchmark, including Qwen3.5-2B which was released just three months ago.
</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900">"Forge Engineering": software built on demand, not designed for generality</h2>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
FaceWall named their approach <strong>Forge Engineering</strong>, and the metaphor is precise.
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
Traditional software engineering follows a factory model: build one general framework, make it compatible with every model, every hardware configuration, every training task. The upside is reusability. The downside is that nothing is truly optimized — it's like buying an off-the-rack suit. It fits everyone adequately, but no one perfectly.
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
Forge Engineering flips this entirely. If AI can write code fast enough and cheap enough, why chase generality? Forge a custom framework for each specific model-hardware-task combination. The AI becomes a tireless master tailor, producing bespoke code for every individual need.
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
This isn't just an engineering innovation. It's a <strong>paradigm shift in how software gets built</strong>. The old mental model was "write once, reuse everywhere." The new model is "generate on demand, optimize specifically, discard when done." Code becomes disposable and hyper-optimized simultaneously.
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
And the implications are staggering: if Forge Engineering works for pre-training frameworks — arguably the most complex systems software in existence — what else can it work for? Operating system kernels? Database engines? Compiler toolchains?
</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900">Meanwhile, a DeepSeek researcher let AI write 99% of his paper</h2>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
While FaceWall was announcing ForgeTrain, DeepSeek researcher Chen Deli quietly published something that might be even more telling about where we're headed.
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
He used his own tool — <strong>DeliAutoResearch</strong> — to write a 46-page survey paper on autonomous research agents. The paper went through six iterations over six days, with approximately 108 agent calls, consuming 648,000 tokens and generating 2,234 lines of LaTeX. All 103 references were manually verified.
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
His contribution? Less than two hours of active thinking time.
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
I want to pause here. This isn't an AI-generated blog post. This is a proper academic survey that proposes an L1-L5 autonomy taxonomy (borrowed from autonomous driving's SAE levels), analyzes four architectural patterns, evaluates 17 systems across six dimensions, and identifies six open research problems. It's the kind of paper that a junior researcher would spend 3-4 weeks writing, and the result is publication-quality.
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
But his own reflection is what stuck with me. He wrote: <strong>"High-intensity work left me too drained to maintain my blog or writing. Now Agents let me pick those back up."</strong>
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
Read that again. For a top-tier researcher at one of the world's most important AI labs, the Agent isn't a tool for writing boilerplate. It's a mechanism for <strong>personal productivity liberation</strong>. It gives him back the creative bandwidth that was consumed by the grinding demands of research execution.
</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900">The flywheel is about to close</h2>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
I started this story thinking I was covering two interesting but separate news items. I ended up seeing something much bigger.
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
We've been incrementally climbing the L1-to-L5 ladder for years. GitHub Copilot (L1) autocompletes your code. Claude Code and Cursor (L3) execute multi-step workflows across your entire codebase. ForgeTrain and DeliAutoResearch (L4) are now producing complete artifacts — frameworks, papers — that would have required dedicated human teams just months ago.
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
The next step is obvious and terrifying in equal measure: <strong>an AI that uses a framework it wrote itself to train a model that is smarter than the AI that wrote the framework.</strong> When that loop closes, you have recursive self-improvement. You have an intelligence flywheel that doesn't need humans to spin it.
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
FaceWall's data suggests the intelligence density of models doubles roughly every 3.5 months. That means in a year, a 1B-parameter model could match today's 5-6B models. In two years, a phone-sized model could deliver what a server rack does today.
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
The implications for the chip war between NVIDIA and Chinese alternatives are equally profound. NVIDIA's deepest moat isn't hardware performance — it's CUDA's 15-year software ecosystem, built by millions of developers. That moat takes time to replicate, and time is what China's chip ecosystem doesn't have. But ForgeTrain points to an alternative path: <strong>don't replicate the ecosystem with human labor, bypass it with AI-generated code.</strong> Let AI automatically produce optimized training and inference frameworks for every new Chinese chip, closing the software gap not through developer communities but through automated generation.
</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900">What I actually think after writing this</h2>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
I've been covering AI for years, and I try hard not to be hyperbolic. But I have to be honest about what I saw this week.
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
This is the first time I've seen a genuine example of an AI system producing something that sits at the very frontier of what human engineers can build — and doing it better. ForgeTrain isn't a toy demo. It's a 10% performance improvement over Megatron, which is the kind of gain that companies spend millions of dollars and years of engineering time chasing.
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
The question isn't whether AI will be able to design AI systems better than humans. It's whether the humans who define the goals, build the harnesses, and judge the output will continue to have a monopoly on those roles.
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
For now, the answer is yes. The L5 autonomous researcher — the one that chooses its own problems, accumulates knowledge across domains, and runs indefinitely without supervision — remains purely theoretical. The key bottlenecks, as Chen Deli's paper explains, are not raw intelligence but metacognition: the AI doesn't know what it doesn't know, and it can't reliably evaluate the novelty or correctness of its own output.
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
But those bottlenecks? They're engineering problems. And increasingly, we're using AI to solve engineering problems.
</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">
The flywheel is spinning. It'll be interesting to see who's still holding on three years from now.
</p>
