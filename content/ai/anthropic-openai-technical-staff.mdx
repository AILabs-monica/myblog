<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">The most valuable AI companies on Earth are quietly eliminating the 200-year-old invention called "middle management."</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">Andrej Karpathy updated his X bio on May 26, 2026. The former Tesla AI director, OpenAI co-founder, and most-cited AI researcher on the planet now describes himself with three letters. MTS. Member of Technical Staff.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">The internet lost it. Someone posted: "Anthropic, you're not actually going to let Karpathy be just a technical staff member, right...?" Karpathy replied himself: "Anthropic is doing the right thing. A huge company with zero corporate bureaucracy. Bunch of MTS, aligned on a mission."</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">But here's what the internet missed. Karpathy isn't alone.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">Instagram co-founder and CTO Mike Krieger? MTS at Anthropic now — he was Chief Product Officer when he joined. Workday CTO Peter Bailis? MTS at Anthropic, doing reinforcement learning. The resumes stacked together could IPO a company. Every single one: "technical staff."</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">And it's not just Anthropic. OpenAI adopted the same system back in 2023. President Greg Brockman said the idea came from Alan Kay at Xerox PARC. Title: Member of Technical Staff. Pay: Department of Labor H-1B data shows $210K–$530K at OpenAI, $300K–$405K at Anthropic. Member in title. Master in compensation.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">I spent three days thinking about this. Here's what I found: they're not doing a "flat org" experiment. They're doing something much more aggressive — they're deleting the entire concept of "managing people" from the organizational genome.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">Middle management was never natural. It was a 200-year workaround.</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">Before the 19th century, nobody was a "manager." You were a blacksmith. You took orders, you forged, you delivered. You didn't need anyone to "manage" you.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">Management was born in factories — when one person couldn't oversee 200 workers, they hired people to do it. Those people did exactly three things:</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;"><strong>Information relay.</strong> Boss says "raise output 20% this month." Manager translates it into "two more boxes per person per day." Worker says "machine broke." Manager translates it into "production delayed three days." Information relay.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;"><strong>Quality gatekeeping.</strong> Worker submits output. Manager checks it. Rejects the bad ones. Quality gatekeeping.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;"><strong>Accountability sponge.</strong> Something goes wrong. Boss yells at manager, not workers. Accountability sponge.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">These three functions held steady for 200 years because they required what technology couldn't provide: instant information transfer, automated quality checks, and a human-shaped liability buffer. Then AI showed up.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;"><strong>Information relay?</strong> Gone. Agents read requirements, decompose tasks, distribute to sub-agents, translate context for each recipient. Zero latency, zero distortion.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;"><strong>Quality gatekeeping?</strong> Gone. AI-native codebases run automated CI, automated code review, automated test coverage gates. Claude Opus 4.8 in Claude Code writes code, runs tests, finds bugs, fixes them, runs tests again — all without a human manager asking "is it ready yet?"</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;"><strong>Accountability sponge?</strong> Still here — but that's not management. That's being a human shield. And it was never the valuable part of the role.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">Three things AI can't consume</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">When task allocation, quality control, and information relay all vanish into AI, something else surfaces. Three capabilities that were always more valuable than management — they were just buried under the busywork.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;"><strong>1. Taste curation.</strong> AI can check whether code coverage hit 80%. It can't tell you "this feature shouldn't be built this way — it's solving a problem users don't have." The first is quality control. The second is taste. Opus 4.8 is four times more honest about bugs in its code than Opus 4.7. But who decided that "honesty" was the quality to optimize for? Who defined what "enough honesty" looks like? That's a taste judgment. Not a computation.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;"><strong>2. Direction anchoring.</strong> AI can decompose "build a payment feature" into 30 sub-tasks. It cannot wake up one morning, look at the roadmap, and say: "Actually, we shouldn't build payments. The real user pain isn't checkout — it's comparison shopping before checkout. We should build the recommendation engine first." That's direction judgment. Not pathfinding — destination-setting.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;"><strong>3. Stop-loss intuition.</strong> A project runs for two months. AI reports: 75% complete, three weeks remaining. AI cannot say: "Everyone, pause. The problem we're chasing doesn't matter to users. These two months might be a sunk cost. I recommend we stop." Optimizers cannot terminate themselves.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">The organization is becoming a lab again</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">MTS wasn't invented by Anthropic. Alan Kay created it at Xerox PARC — a research lab, not a company. PARC didn't have managers. It had a building full of brilliant people, each recognized by what they could solve, not who they reported to. Whoever had the best approach to a problem led that effort.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">The Industrial Revolution buried that model for 200 years. Factories didn't need "whoever has the best approach leads." Factories needed "everyone shows up at 8 AM." AI just made the "show up at 8 AM" part obsolete. Task progress is tracked by agents. Quality is checked by automated pipelines. Status updates are instantaneous dashboards. There is no remaining function that requires a human-shaped relay station between decisions and execution.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">So the organization snaps back to its pre-industrial shape — a network of judgment, not a hierarchy of authority. The question stops being "who reports to whom" and becomes "who has the best read on this problem." This is the real meaning of MTS. Not a cultural experiment. A structural necessity. When AI eliminates coordination overhead, coordination stops being a job. Conviction becomes the job.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">I started this thinking it was about org charts. I found out it's about me.</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">Here's the question I'm left with — and you should ask yourself the same. In your current role: what percentage of your day is allocation, quality checking, and information relay? And what percentage is taste, direction, and stop-loss intuition?</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">If the first category is 80% — you should be worried. Not because AI is coming for you. Because you've spent your career filling a slot that only existed because technology wasn't good enough yet. That slot was never a real job. It was a workaround.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">If the first category is 40% and the rest is arguing with your team about whether the product direction is right, talking to customers about the problem they can't articulate, telling leadership the target needs to change because the market shifted — you're fine. You were never a "manager." You just had a title that said you were.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">Alan Kay said it best: "The best way to predict the future is to invent it." Most people think he meant inventing products. I think he meant inventing the organization itself.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">Xerox PARC invented the graphical interface, the mouse, ethernet, the laser printer. None of those made them the most valuable company on Earth. What they left behind — what Anthropic and OpenAI are now building on — was an answer to a deeper question: "How does a group of brilliant people work together without anyone managing anyone else?"</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">Three letters. MTS.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">Here's the only question that matters for your next decade: does your company need a manager — or does it need the person with the best judgment on the hardest problem?</p>
