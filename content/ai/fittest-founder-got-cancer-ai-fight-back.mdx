<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">I sat down with Conno Christou on a Tuesday afternoon in June. He's 35. He's a founder. He's building Keragon, an AI-powered healthcare automation platform. And he is, by every measurable standard, the kind of person who makes you feel inadequate about your own health decisions.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">He wears a Whoop band on one wrist and an Oura ring on another finger, cross-validating his sleep data across two devices because one might miss something the other catches. Every year, he runs nearly 100 biomarkers through blood work — year over year, tweaking, optimizing, getting better. He follows the protocols of Peter Attia and Rhonda Patrick like other people follow a religion. Supplements. Circadian rhythm. Protein timing. The works.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">His 2025 checkup? All green. Every single number.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">"It was the best I'd had in years," he told me. The way he said it — there was no arrogance in it, just the quiet satisfaction of a man who had done the work and gotten the result he deserved.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">And then, one day after a workout, his arm swelled up.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">He didn't think much of it at first. A week went by before he saw a doctor. Two blood clots, one in each of two veins in his arm. They scheduled surgery. On the day of the pre-op scan, the doctor walked into the room and told him the surgery was off.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">"There's an 11-by-11-by-8-centimeter mass behind your sternum."</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">Biopsy results: aggressive non-Hodgkin's lymphoma. Incidence: roughly 1 in 420,000. Cause: a random genetic mutation. Nothing to do with lifestyle, diet, stress, or anything he could have controlled. Just bad luck. The tumor had been growing for about three months. In another three weeks, his doctors estimated, it would have been stage 4.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">"I was unlucky in the luckiest way," he said. The phrase caught me off guard. Most people, staring down a rare cancer at 35, don't find a way to describe themselves as lucky. But Christou is not most people.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900">Two World-Class Oncologists. Two Completely Opposite Answers.</h2>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">His first oncologist, a widely respected expert, recommended the lighter of two chemotherapy regimens. Christou scheduled his first infusion for three days later.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">The night before that infusion, he went for a second opinion.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">The second doctor didn't hesitate. He recommended the harder path — inpatient infusions, three weeks per cycle, six months total. The reason came down to the specific pathology of Christou's lymphoma subtype.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">The lighter regimen: roughly a <strong class="text-red-600">60%</strong> success rate.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">The harder regimen: roughly <strong class="text-emerald-600">85%</strong>.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">Two experts at the absolute top of their field. Completely opposite judgments. Try to imagine it — you're facing a six-to-seven-month decision that will determine whether you live or die, and two of the best minds in oncology are telling you to walk in two different directions. This is not a "follow your doctor's advice" problem anymore. This is a "you have to figure it out yourself" problem.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">So Christou did what founders do. He activated his network. Over the next two days, he reached every hematologist and oncologist he could find — in the United States, abroad, anyone who would look at his case. <strong>Twelve specialists. Twelve opinions.</strong></p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">Eleven to one. Take the harder chemo.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">He didn't describe this as brave. He described it as rational. He'd been data-driven his entire adult life. The only difference now was that the stakes were his own life.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900">Six Months of Chemo. He Turned It Into a Data Marathon.</h2>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">When Christou was 18, he served 25 months of mandatory military service in Cyprus. During chemo, he borrowed a soldier's mindset: six cycles, each one a finite mission. Get through it. Trust the process. But he was not a soldier who just followed orders. He was a soldier with data.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">Through all six months, he never took off his Whoop band. He discovered something remarkable: the wearable could accurately predict the days his immune system would crash — sometimes before he felt any symptoms at all. He kept a symptom journal using voice transcription, logging every change, every side effect, every drug and counter-drug.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">He compressed his focus down to three variables: sleep quality, nutrition, and — the one he ranked first — mindset.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">"Mindset matters more than anything," he told me. "I never once asked 'why me.' Not once. That question has no useful answer."</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">And then he fed everything — the blood results, the scan data, the wearable output, the journal entries — into Claude.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">You might be thinking: is that safe? You wouldn't be alone. The warnings from medical experts are everywhere, and they're not wrong. Danielle Bitterman, clinical data science and AI lead at Mass General Brigham, told The New York Times that general-purpose chatbots are "frequently wrong" on medical questions and "not thoroughly evaluated" for personalized diagnoses.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">Christou doesn't disagree.</p>

<blockquote class="bg-slate-100 border-l-4 border-violet-500 p-4 my-8 italic text-slate-700 text-lg font-semibold">
"It didn't replace the doctors. It helped me ask the right questions."
</blockquote>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">Sit with that distinction for a second. For a disease as rare as his — the kind an oncologist might see once a year, if that — having a conversation with an AI model that has absorbed the entire corpus of medical literature is fundamentally different from typing symptoms into Google. It's not even the same category of thing.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">A KFF poll from March 2026 found that one in three American adults is now using chatbots for health information and advice. On Reddit, posts are piling up with similar stories — people who spent 25 years bouncing between specialists, getting zero answers, and then an AI model surfaced a plausible direction in minutes.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900">The PET Scan Came Back. And It Wasn't Clean.</h2>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">Six months. Six cycles. Done.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">Christou went in for his final PET scan — the imaging that detects whether active cancer cells remain in the body. He waited for the results the way you wait for anything that might tell you whether you get to live.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">The scan was ambiguous.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">His oncologist began discussing second-line therapy. Radiation, possibly — directed near his heart and lungs.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">I want you to feel what that moment would taste like. You've just endured six months of chemo. You thought the war was over. And then a blurry image tells you it might not be. The next thing you're hearing about is radiotherapy aimed at the organs that keep you alive.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">Christou did what he'd been doing all along. He went back to the research. What he found stopped me cold when he told me. For this specific type of lymphoma, the <strong>false-positive rate on an end-of-treatment PET scan is roughly 60%</strong>.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">Sixty percent. In 2026. A multi-million-dollar imaging machine, used to decide whether you irradiate someone's heart and lungs, is wrong six times out of ten when it says the cancer might still be there.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">He fed all three of his PET scans and his MRI into Claude.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">The model surfaced something his oncologist hadn't considered. A known but easily overlooked phenomenon: in patients under 40 recovering from this specific lymphoma subtype, the thymus gland can reactivate after chemotherapy, lighting up on a PET scan in a pattern that looks exactly like active disease. It's called <strong>thymus rebound</strong> — benign tissue regeneration masquerading as malignancy.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">Based on Christou's age, his specific lymphoma subtype, and the imaging characteristics across all three scans, Claude estimated roughly a <strong>90% probability</strong> that what the PET scan was showing was thymus rebound, not residual cancer.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">Ninety percent. Not cancer.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">Let me say that again, because it matters: an oncologist facing a rare case might not know that the thymus gland can reactivate after chemotherapy in a way that mimics active disease. But an AI model that has absorbed the entire corpus of medical literature can surface that possibility in seconds, quantify it, and hand it to you as a lead to pursue.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">Christou took Claude's analysis to a fourth doctor. The doctor reviewed the scans, the history, the model's reasoning — and confirmed it. Thymus rebound. No active disease. No radiotherapy needed.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed font-semibold text-slate-900">He was clear.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900">AI Didn't Replace the Doctors. It Made the Patient Dangerous.</h2>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">I've been thinking about this story for days now, and the thing that keeps coming back to me isn't how impressive the AI was. It's how many cracks there are in the system.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">Two world-class experts give you completely opposite advice. A diagnostic test with a 60% false-positive rate determines whether you get radiation aimed at your heart and lungs. Life-saving knowledge about a rare disease is scattered across papers all over the world, but your doctor — a brilliant, dedicated human being — might see your specific subtype once a year.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">None of this is anyone's fault. Doctors are not search engines. They're humans operating inside a system that wasn't built for the complexity of modern medicine.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">But in 2026, you no longer have to be a patient who knows only what your doctor tells you.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">This is not an argument that you should replace your doctor with a chatbot. The opposite. AI doesn't give you a diagnosis — it gives you information leverage. It means walking into the clinic with the right vocabulary, the right questions, and an antenna tuned to "what might my doctor, brilliant as they are, have overlooked while managing a hundred other patients?"</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">For Christou's disease, the end-of-treatment PET scan has a 60% false-positive rate. His oncologist was about to irradiate tissue near his heart and lungs. An AI model pointed to thymus rebound. A fourth human doctor confirmed it. If this story had played out five years ago, he would have received unnecessary radiation to his chest.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">I don't know what AI's role in medicine will look like five years from now. Maybe it becomes an officially sanctioned second-opinion mechanism. Maybe regulators crush it. Maybe it becomes standard equipment for every patient, or maybe it stays a privilege reserved for founders with technical fluency and deep networks.</p>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">But here's what I do know, after hearing this story: we finally have a tool that lets an ordinary person, in their most vulnerable moment, facing a complex system they don't understand, stop being entirely passive. This tool is still fallible. The experts' warnings are real. But when a patient's options are "an AI that might be wrong" and "a human system that is demonstrably wrong 60% of the time on a critical test" — and she has the right to use both, cross-checking one against the other — I believe that is already progress.</p>

<blockquote class="bg-slate-100 border-l-4 border-violet-500 p-4 my-8 text-slate-700 text-lg font-semibold">
"It's not happening in 10 years. It's happening today."
</blockquote>

<p class="mb-4 leading-relaxed">I believe him.</p>
