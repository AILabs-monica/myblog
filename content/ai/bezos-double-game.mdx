<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">I read the news on Friday night and had to check the date to make sure it wasn't April 1st. Jeff Bezos' robotics company, Prometheus, just raised $12 billion — in a single round. Not a public offering. Not a debt issuance. A private funding round for a company with no public product, no disclosed revenue, and a website so sparse it could fit on a napkin. For context, the entire global industrial robotics market is about $20 billion per year. Prometheus just absorbed 60% of a global industry's annual revenue in one gulp.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">The Wall Street Journal, The Information, and Reuters all reported within 24 hours that Amazon CEO Andy Jassy had personally called US Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent to warn that Anthropic's Claude Fable 5 model could be jailbroken for cyberattacks. Within 48 hours, the US government invoked export control authority and ordered Anthropic to shut down Fable 5 and Mythos 5 — globally. Oh, and Amazon is Anthropic's largest investor. $5 billion in equity. $100 billion in committed cloud spending. You invested in the company, and then your CEO personally called the government to burn its flagship product to the ground.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">The Contradiction That Makes No Sense — Until It Does</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">On the surface, Amazon's actions are self-destructive. Why get your portfolio company's flagship product banned? The answer requires understanding what Anthropic's Mythos model actually does. When Anthropic unveiled Mythos in April, the model had found security vulnerabilities in every major operating system and browser tested. Anthropic created Project Glasswing — a controlled program sharing Mythos with roughly 50 vetted organizations including Amazon. Amazon researchers, as part of Glasswing, discovered that Fable 5 could be jailbroken to extract cyberattack-relevant information. Instead of reporting this back to Anthropic through established channels, Andy Jassy went straight to the Treasury Secretary.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">David Sacks, former AI czar, confirmed: <strong>"A highly credible trusted partner came forward with a jailbreak. The Admin asked Dario to fix it or de-deploy the model. Dario refused."</strong> By acting as the "responsible partner" who reported a vulnerability, Amazon positioned itself on the side of the government while simultaneously eliminating its biggest competitive threat in enterprise AI. The fact that Amazon is an Anthropic investor only makes the knife twist more painfully: Jassy used his insider access as an investor to discover a vulnerability, then used that discovery to trigger a government takedown.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">The Irony: Anthropic's Safety Branding Became Its Undoing</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">For six months, Anthropic staked its entire public identity on being the "safety-first" AI company. Every press release screamed: "We build AI so powerful it requires extraordinary precautions." Sam Altman saw this coming. In April, he called Anthropic's approach <strong>"fear-based marketing"</strong>: "It is clearly incredible marketing to say, 'We have built a bomb. We were about to drop it on your head.'" When you spend six months telling the world your AI is uniquely dangerous, the world listens. And when someone like Andy Jassy comes to the government with evidence that your dangerous AI can be jailbroken, the government doesn't ask for a peer-reviewed paper. It acts.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">The $12 Billion Bet</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">Prometheus is described as building an <strong>"artificial general engineer"</strong> — a robot that can perform physical tasks across manufacturing, warehousing, construction, and agriculture. Fanuc, the world's largest industrial robotics company, does about $5 billion in annual revenue. The entire industrial robotics market is roughly $20 billion. Prometheus just raised $12 billion in a single round for a company that hasn't shipped a single commercial product. This tells you what the smartest capital in the world believes: within a decade, AI-powered physical labor will be a market measured in trillions.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">The Money Tells the Story: In June 2026 alone: Alphabet is planning an $80 billion stock sale for AI. Amazon raised $31.5 billion in 48 hours. Meta issued its largest-ever bond at $30 billion. Prometheus raised $12 billion. That's over $150 billion in one month. This isn't normal capital raising. This is what happens when the people with the most information and resources believe something fundamental has shifted.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mt-10 mb-4 text-slate-900" style="font-size:15px;">What This Means For Everyone Else</h2>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">First, the AI disruption arc is closing. Prometheus's raise signals that physical labor is next. Second, competition is moving beyond products — AI competition now includes a political dimension most companies are unprepared for. Third, Bezos now has simultaneous leverage over digital AI (AWS + Anthropic), physical AI (Prometheus), and government AI policy (Jassy-Treasury connection). Nobody else has all three.</p>

<p class="mb-4" style="font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;">The Question No One Is Asking: What happens when the people building AI also control the government mechanisms that regulate it? Jassy didn't file a lawsuit. He picked up the phone and called the Treasury Secretary. Within 48 hours, the most advanced AI model was dead. This is empire-building at a speed and scale we've never seen before.</p>
